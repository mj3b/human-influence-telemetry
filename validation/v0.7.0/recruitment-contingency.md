# HIT v0.7.0 Recruitment Contingency and Evidentiary Downgrade Plan

**Protocol:** `HIT-IRP-HIT040-002`  
**Status:** Pre-lock contingency candidate  
**Primary design:** Three eligible independent scorers assess all three frozen packets  
**Current protocol status:** Candidate; scoring prohibited

## 1. Decision

Failure to recruit the declared independent scorers does not have a zero-rater substitute.

Inter-rater reliability is evidence about variation across different human raters applying the same instrument to the same objects. Author re-scoring, model scoring, deterministic validation, synthetic fixtures, and a single clean-room reviewer may produce useful evidence, but they answer different research questions.

Accordingly:

1. the original three-scorer design remains the preferred study;
2. missing recruitment may delay or redesign the study, but may not be represented as successful H3 replication;
3. any reduced-rater design must be specified before substantive scoring begins, use a numbered amendment or new protocol identifier, recalculate all denominators and thresholds, and publish the recruitment failure that caused the change;
4. zero independent scorers means no current-contract inter-rater result and no Maturity Level 3 decision under `HIT-IRP-HIT040-002`;
5. other research tracks may proceed, but their claims must remain separated from human reliability.

## 2. Why no direct substitute exists

The current research question asks whether independent reviewers can apply HIT reproducibly. That claim contains an essential causal variable: reviewer identity.

Replacing independent reviewers with any of the following removes that variable:

- repeated ratings by the author;
- repeated ratings by one external reviewer;
- ratings generated by a language model;
- deterministic rules or schema validation;
- adjudicated consensus without preserved independent ratings;
- author-created gold-standard answers.

Those methods can test stability, usability, implementation conformance, ambiguity, or criterion alignment. They cannot estimate variation among independent reviewers.

## 3. Evidence map

| Design | Evidence produced | Valid claim | Does not support |
|---|---|---|---|
| Three independent scorers across three packets | Cross-rater, cross-case application evidence | Bounded H3 replication under the declared protocol | Population-wide reliability or validity |
| Two independent scorers across three packets | Reduced inter-rater pilot | Pairwise reproducibility under a revised protocol | The existing 72-comparison threshold or full three-rater design |
| Two scorers per packet with different scorer pools | Packet-level inter-rater evidence | Reproducibility for each packet | Same-rater transfer across evidence conditions |
| Third scorer triggered by predeclared disagreement | Adaptive inter-rater evidence | Efficient disagreement resolution under a new sequential design | Results under the current fixed design |
| One independent clean-room reviewer | External applicability and usability evidence | A stranger can operate the public package | Inter-rater reliability |
| Author blind re-score after washout | Intra-rater stability | Author scoring is stable across occasions | Independent application |
| Synthetic and mutation testing | Executable E1 evidence | Rules and failure states are implemented deterministically | Human interpretation reliability |
| Model stress testing | Adversarial ambiguity and tooling evidence | Candidate contract defects and prompt sensitivity | Human reliability or valid substantive findings |
| Prospective institutional pilot | Ecological application evidence | Real-world use under a declared protocol | Automatic H3 success unless multiple independent reviewers are preserved |

## 4. Contingency ladder

### Tier A: Preserve the current protocol

Use this tier while three eligible scorers remain realistically obtainable.

Permitted actions:

- publish `v0.7.0` with the locked protocol and frozen packets;
- open a public asynchronous scoring call;
- conduct targeted outreach to governance, audit, law, policy, assurance, and socio-technical research communities;
- offer a disclosed fixed honorarium that is independent of findings;
- accept rolling expressions of interest while keeping submissions sealed;
- provide only access and schema-format support;
- extend the recruitment window without changing the research question.

Compensation does not by itself destroy independence. The relevant risks are contribution to the method or packet, exposure to expected findings, conflicts of interest, coordination with other scorers, and payment contingent on substantive outcomes.

### Tier B: Reduce participant burden without reducing the number of raters

This is the preferred operational fallback.

Possible redesigns, all requiring a pre-scoring amendment or new protocol:

1. **Packet-specialist design:** recruit three independent scorers per packet, but each person scores only one packet. This requires nine people but greatly reduces individual burden.
2. **Partially crossed design:** recruit a larger pool in which each scorer assesses two packets and each packet receives at least three independent ratings. This preserves some cross-case overlap while reducing individual workload.
3. **Staged packet release:** score one packet at a time while keeping its results sealed until the declared study gate is reached.

These designs can be analyzed with methods that permit incomplete or varying rater assignment, but they test a different generalization structure from three common scorers rating all three packets.

### Tier C: Reduced-rater pilot

Use only when three-scorer recruitment is infeasible but at least two eligible scorers can be recruited.

Required controls:

- create `HIT-IRP-HIT040-003` or a numbered pre-lock amendment;
- define two-scorer primary measures and thresholds before any ratings are unsealed;
- remove the three-rater unanimity measure;
- treat Krippendorff's alpha and chance-corrected measures as supplementary and interpret them cautiously with a small sample;
- publish the recruitment failure and the design change;
- label the result a pilot, not the planned H3 replication;
- retain Maturity Level 2 unless the maturity model is prospectively revised for reasons independent of the observed scores.

A two-rater design still produces genuine inter-rater evidence. It produces less information about rater heterogeneity and does not satisfy the current protocol as written.

### Tier D: Adaptive third-rater design

This can reduce expected scoring burden while preserving a route to three-rater evidence.

Candidate structure:

1. two independent scorers assess each packet;
2. a third scorer is recruited only for packet-items meeting a predeclared trigger, such as any critical disagreement, any `IE` versus determinate disagreement, or packet agreement below a declared threshold;
3. all two-rater results remain primary and public;
4. the third rating is reported as a sequential extension, not retroactively folded into an unchanged fixed-design claim.

The stopping rule, trigger, estimator, denominator, and interpretation must be registered before scoring. Sequential reliability designs can reduce average sample requirements, but they cannot be improvised after inspecting results.

### Tier E: No viable independent scorers

When zero independent scorers can be recruited, close the human replication as recruitment-incomplete and publish that fact.

Continue only research tracks that do not require independent raters:

- complete three current-contract author applications with explicit author-scored status;
- perform a clean-room installation and usability audit when one external technical reviewer becomes available;
- expand executable conformance and mutation testing;
- conduct formal ambiguity and decision-boundary analysis;
- map HIT evidence to adjacent frameworks without claiming equivalence;
- prepare a prospective institutional pilot;
- publish a recruitment report documenting outreach, eligibility failures, burden, compensation offered, and response rates.

Consequences:

- H3 remains supported only for the earlier bounded `0.1.0` Cigna exercise;
- current-contract H3 replication remains unresolved;
- Maturity Level 3 remains pending under the present maturity rules;
- the repository may advance technically, but not by relabeling technical evidence as independent human evidence.

## 5. Strongest practical alternative

The strongest fallback is not model scoring. It is an **open, asynchronous, compensated replication challenge** using the frozen public packets.

Recommended structure:

1. publish scorer-ready packets with stable hashes;
2. publish eligibility and conflict criteria;
3. publish the fixed honorarium before applications are reviewed;
4. screen eligibility without exposing expected findings;
5. accept submissions asynchronously and keep them sealed;
6. stop only when the predeclared required scorer structure is reached or the recruitment-closure rule is triggered;
7. publish both the scoring result and recruitment funnel.

This converts recruitment from private persuasion into a public reproducibility test. It also creates evidence about whether the method is practically usable by strangers.

## 6. Recruitment-closure rule candidate

Before protocol lock, define a closure rule based on completed outreach rounds rather than an arbitrary calendar date.

A candidate rule is:

- Round 1: direct invitations to qualified governance, audit, legal, policy, and assurance practitioners;
- Round 2: public repository call and professional-network circulation;
- Round 3: disclosed honorarium and reduced scheduling friction;
- Round 4: institutional and academic outreach for research-assistant or practicum participation.

After all four rounds, the coordinator publishes:

- invitations issued;
- responses;
- eligibility outcomes;
- accepted scorers;
- withdrawals;
- stated burden concerns;
- conflicts and contamination exclusions;
- whether the original, amended, or recruitment-incomplete path applies.

No person is counted as a scorer until a complete, verified submission is received.

## 7. Protocol-change rule

Any recruitment-driven change must occur before the affected substantive ratings are unsealed.

The change record must state:

- external event causing the change;
- original and revised scorer design;
- revised estimands and denominators;
- revised thresholds;
- loss of generalizability;
- effect on H3 and maturity decisions;
- whether the original protocol is withdrawn, amended, or retained as incomplete;
- whether collected submissions remain usable under the revised design.

Changes are not evidence of failure when disclosed prospectively. Quietly changing the design after observing scores would make the confirmatory claim uninterpretable.

## 8. Non-substitution rules

The following are prohibited as replacements for missing independent scorers:

- counting the HIT author as an independent scorer;
- counting the same person under multiple pseudonyms or scoring occasions;
- counting model outputs as human submissions;
- converting author assessments into independent submissions;
- using consensus ratings without preserving independent pre-consensus ratings;
- lowering thresholds after results are observed;
- removing a packet or scorer because agreement is low;
- declaring Maturity Level 3 from three author-scored applications alone;
- treating schema validity as substantive reliability;
- treating a clean-room usability pass as inter-rater agreement.

## 9. Methodological basis

- Hallgren, K. A. (2012), *Computing Inter-Rater Reliability for Observational Data: An Overview and Tutorial*, explains that inter-rater reliability evaluates consistency among ratings supplied by multiple coders: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3402032/
- Koo, T. K., and Li, M. Y. (2016), *A Guideline of Selecting and Reporting Intraclass Correlation Coefficients for Reliability Research*, distinguishes inter-rater reliability from intra-rater and test-retest reliability: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4913118/
- Marzi, G., Balzano, M., and Marchiori, D. (2024), *K-Alpha Calculator*, describes Krippendorff's alpha as accommodating varying numbers of raters and missing data: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11636850/
- Ten Hove, D., Jorgensen, T. D., and van der Ark, L. A. (2022), *Updated guidelines on selecting an intraclass correlation coefficient for interrater reliability, with applications to incomplete observational designs*, addresses designs in which raters vary across subjects: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36048052/
- Xie, M., Singh, K., and Zhang, C.-H. (2014), *Sequential Testing of Measurement Errors in Inter-Rater Reliability Studies*, develops group-sequential reliability procedures with lower average sample requirements: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25525316/
- Center for Open Science guidance states that preregistered plans may be transparently updated for events outside the researchers' control, with justification and explicit disclosure of deviations: https://help.osf.io/article/113-advanced-actions-registrations
